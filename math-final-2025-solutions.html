QUESTION 1 — ACCURACY & NON-LINEAR EQUATIONS
QUESTION 1 [20 MARKS]

a) Given f(x) = 4x³ − 2x² + 1: (i) find the exact value of f(2.13). (ii) find the absolute and relative error if f(2.13) is rounded to 3 decimal points.

b) Find the root of f(x) = x³ − 4x + 1 to 1 decimal point accuracy in [1, 3] using the Bisection method. Calculate to 3 decimal points.

c) Find the root to 2 decimal points for f(x) = cos(x) − x using the Secant method, where x₀ = 1 and x₁ = 1.5. Calculate to 4 decimal points.

🧠 FUNDAMENTAL CONCEPT — start here if any of this is new to you

What "error" actually means (part a): a calculator can only store a finite number of digits, so the instant you round 30.580588 down to 30.581 you've thrown information away — that thrown-away sliver IS the error. Two ways to describe it: absolute error is just "how far off is my rounded number, in raw units" (you literally subtract). Relative error asks the same question but scaled against the size of the true answer — being off by 1 is catastrophic if the true answer is 2, but meaningless if the true answer is 2,000,000. Dividing the absolute error by the true value puts every error "in proportion", which is why it's usually read as a percentage.

What a "root" is (parts b & c): the root of f(x) is simply an x-value where the graph of f touches or crosses the x-axis — i.e. where f(x)=0. For a straight line you can solve this in one algebra step. For a cubic like x³−4x+1, a closed-form formula exists but it's long, ugly, and easy to botch by hand — so instead of solving exactly, we hunt for the root numerically: start with a rough guess (or two), and repeatedly refine it until we land close enough.

Why the sign-check trick actually works (the Intermediate Value Theorem, "IVT"): picture walking along the graph of f from x=a to x=b, pen never leaving the paper (that no-jumps, no-teleporting property is exactly what "continuous" means). If you start below the x-axis (f(a) < 0 — imagine you're standing in a valley, underwater) and finish above it (f(b) > 0 — now you're on a hilltop), your pen was FORCED to cross the x-axis at least once somewhere along the way — there's no way to get from underwater to a hilltop without passing through sea level at some point. That's the whole theorem. It's the reason Bisection is allowed to say "a root exists somewhere in (a,b)" the moment it sees f(a) and f(b) have opposite signs — no further proof needed.

Bisection = the "higher or lower?" guessing game: exactly like guessing a number between 1 and 100 by always asking "higher or lower than 50?", Bisection takes the current bracket [a,b], calculates the midpoint c, and asks "does the sign-change (and therefore the root) live in the left half [a,c] or the right half [c,b]?" — then throws away the half that CAN'T contain it. Every round the search space is cut exactly in half, which is why it's slow but never fails: it's a brute-force search, not a clever guess.

Secant = "pretend the curve is a straight line for a moment": computing the TRUE instantaneous slope of cos(x)−x at a point requires calculus (differentiation) redone by hand every single iteration — tedious and easy to get wrong under exam pressure. Secant sidesteps this entirely: it takes your two most recent guesses, draws a straight line through the two points (x₀,f(x₀)) and (x₁,f(x₁)) — this straight line is called a "secant line", literally meaning a line that cuts through two points of a curve — and then asks "if the curve really WERE this straight line, where would it cross zero?" That crossing point (simple algebra for a straight line) becomes your new, sharper guess. Right near the actual root, a smooth curve looks almost exactly like a straight line anyway, which is why this crude-sounding trick converges faster than Bisection in practice — though, unlike Bisection, nothing guarantees it will converge at all (a badly-chosen starting pair can make it wander off or even diverge).

🎯 HOW TO APPROACH IT

(a) Straight substitution, then apply the absolute/relative error formulas from Chapter 5 — no iteration needed.

(b) Verify IVT first (always), then bisect using x_c=(a+b)/2, tracking a, b, f(a), f(c) every round. Stop once the digit at the target decimal place stabilises across consecutive iterates.

(c) Secant needs no sign-change bracket — just two starting points and the formula from the sheet. Always slide the window forward (drop the oldest point) each round.

Suppose the true value was π = 3.14159265… and you round it to 3.14 (2 d.p.). The absolute error is just true − approx = 3.14159265 − 3.14 = 0.00159265 — literally the size of the gap between the two numbers on a number line, no scaling involved.

Notice the absolute error (0.0016) looks tiny either way here — but relative error is what lets you compare fairly across wildly different scales: an absolute error of 0.0016 is fine if you're measuring in kilometres, but enormous if you're measuring in nanometres. QUESTION 2 — EIGENVALUES & EIGENVECTORS
QUESTION 2 [20 MARKS]

a) Given A = [[4,1],[2,3]]: (i) use the characteristic polynomial to find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors. (ii) show Σλᵢ = Σaᵢᵢ (trace).

b) Given B = [[4,1,−1],[2,3,1],[−1,1,2]]: (i) find the smallest-magnitude eigenvalue λ₃ and its eigenvector using the shifted power method, given the largest eigenvalue λ₁ = 5, starting v₀ = [0.5, −0.4, 0.5]ᵀ, iterate until ε = 0.05. (ii) find the intermediate eigenvalue λ₂.

🧠 FUNDAMENTAL CONCEPT — start here if any of this is new to you

What a matrix actually DOES: think of any vector as an arrow starting at the origin. When you multiply that vector by a matrix A, you get a NEW arrow out — the matrix has transformed it, usually both rotating it (changing its direction) AND stretching or squashing it (changing its length). Try it on almost any vector and it comes out pointing somewhere completely different.

What an eigenvector is: for most matrices, a handful of very special directions exist where the matrix does NOT rotate the vector at all — it only stretches or shrinks it (or flips it to point the exact opposite way), leaving it pointing along the SAME line it started on. Those special directions are the eigenvectors, and the stretch/shrink factor for each one is its eigenvalue (λ). In symbols: Av = λv means "A acting on v gives back the same v, just scaled by λ" — no rotation whatsoever.

Where the characteristic polynomial comes from: we want to find v (nonzero) and λ satisfying Av = λv, i.e. (A − λI)v = 0. A matrix equation Mv=0 has a NONZERO solution v only when M "collapses" some direction down to zero-length — geometrically, when M squashes space so hard it loses a whole dimension. The determinant of a matrix measures exactly this: det(M) is the factor by which M scales areas/volumes, and det(M)=0 means M crushes at least one direction flat to nothing. So setting det(A − λI) = 0 and expanding it gives a polynomial equation in λ (the characteristic polynomial) — its roots are precisely the λ values for which such a collapsing, zero-sending direction exists. Those roots ARE the eigenvalues.

Why repeated multiplication finds the BIGGEST eigenvalue (the power method): here's a number analogy first — if you repeatedly multiply 3ⁿ against 2ⁿ, the ratio 3ⁿ/2ⁿ = 1.5ⁿ grows without bound as n increases, so 3ⁿ utterly dominates 2ⁿ for large n even though 3 and 2 aren't that different to start with. The exact same thing happens to vectors: almost ANY starting vector v₀ can be described as a mixture ("blend") of all the matrix's eigen-directions. Every time you multiply by A, the component lying along the eigenvector with the LARGEST |λ| gets amplified more than every other component (because it's being multiplied by a bigger number, over and over, compounding exponentially like 3ⁿ vs 2ⁿ above). After enough rounds, that one dominant direction completely swamps all the others, and your vector ends up pointing (almost) exactly along the dominant eigenvector — regardless of which random-ish vector you started with. Dividing by the largest entry each round ("normalising") is just housekeeping — it stops the numbers exploding to infinity (or shrinking to zero) so you can clearly watch the scaling factor m settle down to the eigenvalue.

The "shift" trick, in plain terms: subtracting λ₁ from every diagonal entry of A (forming A − λ₁I) shifts EVERY eigenvalue down by exactly λ₁, while leaving all the eigenVECTORS completely unchanged. If λ₁ was the largest eigenvalue of A, then after the shift it becomes λ₁−λ₁ = 0, which is no longer the biggest in absolute value — whichever eigenvalue was originally SMALLEST is now often the largest in magnitude (especially if it was negative). Running the exact same power-method trick on this shifted matrix therefore homes in on that eigenvalue instead — you just have to remember to add λ₁ back at the very end to undo the shift.

🎯 HOW TO APPROACH IT

(a) For a 2×2 matrix, use the fast shortcut λ² − (trace)λ + det = 0 instead of expanding the full determinant — much less room for error. This shortcut ONLY works for 2×2 — for a 3×3 like B in part (b), you'd have to expand the full det(B−λI)=0 cubic by hand (slow, error-prone), which is exactly why the exam instead asks for the Power Method — a numerical shortcut that sidesteps the cubic entirely.

(b)(i) "Shifted power method" here means: form A_shifted = B − λ₁I (subtract the GIVEN largest eigenvalue from the diagonal), then run the ORDINARY power method on A_shifted. Its dominant eigenvalue m corresponds to (λ₃ − λ₁), so recover λ₃ = m + λ₁ at the end.

(b)(ii) Don't re-run another iteration — use Σλᵢ = trace(B): λ₂ = trace(B) − λ₁ − λ₃.

🔍 THE LONG WAY FIRST — where λ²−7λ+10=0 actually comes from

Before using the shortcut, see the raw derivation once: A − λI subtracts λ from each diagonal entry: A − λI = [[4−λ, 1], [2, 3−λ]]. Setting det(A−λI)=0 for a 2×2 matrix [[p,q],[r,s]] means ps − qr = 0:

(4−λ)(3−λ) − (1)(2) = 0

12 − 4λ − 3λ + λ² − 2 = 0 → λ² − 7λ + 10 = 0

Notice the λ coefficient (−7) is just −(4+3) = −trace(A), and the constant (+10) is exactly det(A) = 4×3 − 1×2. That's not a coincidence — for ANY 2×2 matrix, expanding det(A−λI) always produces exactly λ² − (trace)λ + det, which is why the shortcut below is always safe to use instead of re-deriving this expansion every time.

(a)(i) CHARACTERISTIC POLYNOMIAL (using the shortcut)

trace(A) = 4+3 = 7  ·  det(A) = 4(3)−1(2) = 10

λ² − 7λ + 10 = 0 → (λ−5)(λ−2) = 0 → λ₁ = 5, λ₂ = 2

Eigenvector for λ=5: (A−5I)v=0 → [[−1,1],[2,−2]]v=0 → −v₁+v₂=0 → v=t(1,1)

Eigenvector for λ=2: (A−2I)v=0 → [[2,1],[2,1]]v=0 → 2v₁+v₂=0 → v=t(1,−2)

✓ FINAL ANSWER (a)(i)

λ=5 with eigenvector (1,1)  ·  λ=2 with eigenvector (1,−2)

(a)(ii) TRACE THEOREM CHECK

Σλᵢ = 5+2 = 7  ·  Σaᵢᵢ = trace(A) = 4+3 = 7  ·  7 = 7 ✓ theorem holds

(b)(i) SET UP THE SHIFT

A_shifted = B − 5I = [[−1,1,−1],[2,−2,1],[−1,1,−3]]

Now apply the ORDINARY power method (largest-magnitude component each round) to this shifted matrix.

🔍 HOW ROW k=1 IS BUILT — the matrix multiplication by hand

Each round is 3 mechanical steps: (1) multiply A_shifted by the current vector, (2) extract m = the entry with the largest absolute value, (3) normalise by dividing every entry by m. Here's k=1 in full, using v₀=(0.5, −0.4, 0.5)ᵀ:

Step 1 — multiply (row · column, one row of A_shifted at a time):

Row 1: (−1)(0.5) + (1)(−0.4) + (−1)(0.5) = −0.5 − 0.4 − 0.5 = −1.400

Row 2: (2)(0.5) + (−2)(−0.4) + (1)(0.5) = 1.0 + 0.8 + 0.5 = 2.300

Row 3: (−1)(0.5) + (1)(−0.4) + (−3)(0.5) = −0.5 − 0.4 − 1.5 = −2.400

→ u₁ = (−1.400, 2.300, −2.400)

QUESTION 3 — INTERPOLATION & LEAST SQUARES
QUESTION 3 [20 MARKS]

An object's position is recorded: t = 1,2,3,4,5 → position = 2.0, 3.5, 7.8, 13.9, 21.0 (metres).

a) Estimate the position at t = 4.4 using (i) the appropriate Newton method, and (ii) the Lagrange interpolating polynomial (L₀=−0.03, L₁=−0.19, L₂=−0.49 given).

b) Find p(x) = a₀ + a₁x using Least Squares Approximation. (All calculations to 2 d.p.)

🧠 FUNDAMENTAL CONCEPT — start here if any of this is new to you

What "interpolation" means: you have a table of measurements taken at specific moments (t=1,2,3,4,5) but you want to know the value at a moment you DIDN'T measure (t=4.4, which sits BETWEEN two known points). Interpolation means estimating that in-between value using a smooth curve drawn through the known points — as opposed to "extrapolation", which means guessing OUTSIDE the range you measured (much riskier, since you have no evidence the pattern continues).

Why there's exactly one "right" polynomial: a remarkable fact from algebra — give any n+1 points with distinct x-values, and there is exactly ONE polynomial of degree ≤ n that passes through every single one of them exactly. With 5 points here, that's exactly one degree-4 polynomial. Newton's difference formulas and Lagrange's formula are simply two different sets of arithmetic bookkeeping for CONSTRUCTING/EVALUATING that one same polynomial — like two different routes to the same destination on a map. Because it's the same underlying curve, both methods are mathematically guaranteed to output the identical number if done correctly (which is exactly why 16.66 showed up both times above — that agreement isn't a coincidence, it's a proof neither calculation has an arithmetic error).

Why Newton needs a "difference table" at all: instead of solving a messy system of 5 equations in 5 unknowns (the brute-force way to fit a degree-4 polynomial), Newton's method builds the polynomial one small, simple piece at a time using only SUBTRACTIONS of neighbouring table values — far less arithmetic, and it naturally tells you which few points matter most for your specific target x (the ones nearest it).

Least squares solves a completely different problem. Real, measured data (think: readings from a wobbly instrument, or noisy experimental results) almost never lies EXACTLY on any clean polynomial — there's random scatter. Forcing an exact-fit curve through every noisy point would produce a wild, wiggly curve that just chases the noise (this is called "overfitting") and is useless for predicting anything. Instead, least squares asks a more modest, more useful question: "what's the SIMPLEST shape (here, a straight line p(x)=a₀+a₁x) that sits closest, on average, to ALL the points at once?" It's a best compromise, not an exact fit — and it deliberately does NOT pass through most of the actual data points.

Why "squared" distance specifically (and not just distance): for each point, the "residual" is the vertical gap between the real y-value and the line's predicted value. If you just added up the plain gaps, positive gaps (line too low) and negative gaps (line too high) could cancel each other out, hiding a genuinely bad fit that happens to average to zero. Squaring every residual before adding (1) makes every term positive so nothing cancels, and (2) punishes big misses far more than small ones (a gap of 4 contributes 16, not 4) — pushing the line to avoid any single wildly-wrong point. Squaring also happens to make the minimisation solvable cleanly with calculus (set the derivative of the total squared error to zero), which is exactly where the "normal equations" formulas for a₀ and a₁ below come from.

🎯 HOW TO APPROACH IT

(a)(i) t=4.4 sits near the END of the table (closest to t=5) → backward difference is the "appropriate" Newton method here (forward would need many terms to converge from the far end).

(a)(ii) With 5 data points, Lagrange uses ALL FIVE (degree 4) — the 3 L-values given are a hint/check, not the full set; you must derive L₃ and L₄ yourself. Sanity check: all Lᵢ(x) must always sum to exactly 1 — use this to catch arithmetic slips.

(b) Standard linear least-squares normal equations using all 5 (t, position) pairs.

🔍 HOW THE TABLE ABOVE IS BUILT — pure subtraction

A difference table has no hidden formula — every entry is just the value BELOW minus the value ABOVE it in the previous column: Δ¹ = subtract adjacent y's (3.50−2.00=1.50, 7.80−3.50=4.30, 13.90−7.80=6.10, 21.00−13.90=7.10). Δ² = subtract adjacent Δ¹ entries (4.30−1.50=2.80, 6.10−4.30=1.80, 7.10−6.10=1.00). Keep going and the columns shrink by one each time, forming a staircase that narrows to a point.

For the backward formula specifically, you always harvest the BOTTOM-most number of each column (21.00, 7.10, 1.00, −0.80, 0.20 above) because those sit closest to x=5 — the anchor point nearest your target t=4.4. 🔍 THE MASTER FORMULA — where every number above comes from

QUESTION 4 — NUMERICAL DIFFERENTIATION
QUESTION 4 [20 MARKS]

Table 2 gives f(x) = ln(x²+1) at x = 0.9 to 1.4 (step 0.1): f = 0.58779, 0.69315, 0.78846, 0.87547, 0.95551, 1.02962.

a) Estimate f′(1.0) with h=0.1 using (i) two-point forward, (ii) three-point forward, (iii) five-point forward.

b) Given f′(x) = 2x/(x²+1) exactly, find the absolute error for each estimate in (a) and explain what more data points do to accuracy.

c) Estimate f″(1.1) using the five-point central difference formula, h=0.1.

🧠 FUNDAMENTAL CONCEPT — start here if any of this is new to you

What a derivative actually is: f′(x) is the SLOPE of the curve at exactly the point x — how steeply, and in which direction, f is rising or falling right there. Physically it's a rate of change (e.g. speed is the derivative of position: how fast position is changing at this instant). Formally, it's defined as a LIMIT: f′(x) = lim(h→0) [f(x+h) − f(x)] / h — take a nearby point a tiny distance h away, measure the slope of the straight line to it, then imagine squeezing h down to literally zero.

Why we can't just "use the definition": the definition needs h→0, but here we only have a TABLE of f-values measured at fixed spacing (h=0.1 apart) — there's no way to make h smaller than the gap between rows, because no data exists in between. So instead of taking a true limit, we accept a small but FINITE h (0.1) and get an APPROXIMATE slope — this is the entire idea of "numerical" differentiation: trade perfect accuracy for something computable from real, finite data.

Taylor series, in plain words: this is the key tool that makes the approximation possible. It says any smooth function's value a little further along, f(x+h), can be rebuilt from everything you know AT x: f(x+h) = f(x) + h·f′(x) + (h²/2)·f″(x) + (h³/6)·f‴(x) + … — the further terms matter less and less because h is small and h², h³, h⁴ shrink fast. This is just a more precise version of "if I know my current position and speed, I can predict roughly where I'll be a moment later" — and the extra terms are corrections for acceleration, and corrections-to-the-corrections, and so on.

Where the finite-difference formulas come from: write out the Taylor expansion for f(x+h), f(x+2h), f(x+3h), f(x+4h) (four separate equations, all in terms of the unknowns f′(x), f″(x), f‴(x), …). Then find the specific combination of these four equations that makes the f″, f‴, and f⁗ terms all cancel out, leaving an equation you can solve cleanly for f′(x) alone in terms of ONLY the table's f-values. That combination is exactly the "2-point", "3-point", "5-point" formulas — the coefficients (−25, 48, −36, 16, −3, etc.) are simply the numbers that make that cancellation work out. The terms that DON'T fully cancel (because you stopped combining after using only 2, 3, or 5 points) are the leftover "truncation error" — and using MORE points cancels MORE of those leftover terms, which is precisely why 2-point → 3-point → 5-point gets dramatically more accurate.

All the data you need (x=1.0 to 1.4 for part a/b, x=0.9 to 1.3 for part c) already exists in Table 2 — no extra values need computing here. Just plug straight into each named formula from the formula sheet.

(b) Compare each estimate against the exact f′(1.0)=2(1)/(1+1)=1.0 — expect the error to shrink noticeably as you use more points (higher-order formula).

QUESTION 5 — NUMERICAL INTEGRATION
QUESTION 5 [20 MARKS]

Approximate ∫₁⁴ [(x²−x+1)/x³] dx using: a) Trapezoidal, N=6. b) Simpson's 1/3, N=6. c) Romberg integration, calculate until R₃,₃.

🧠 FUNDAMENTAL CONCEPT — start here if any of this is new to you

What an integral actually means: ∫f(x)dx over [1,4] is the exact AREA trapped between the curve y=f(x) and the x-axis, from x=1 to x=4. For some functions you can find an exact "antiderivative" formula and evaluate it at the two endpoints to get this area precisely, in one line. For many real functions — including this one — that clean formula either doesn't exist in elementary form or is impractical to use, so instead we APPROXIMATE the area using shapes we already know how to measure exactly.

The core trick — replace the curve with simple shapes: chop the interval [1,4] into N narrow vertical strips of equal width h. Each strip is almost a rectangle, except its top edge follows the curve, not a flat line. If we swap that curvy top for something simpler — whose area formula we already know — we get a usable estimate. Add up all N strip-areas and that total approximates the whole integral.

Trapezoidal rule: replace each strip's curvy top with a straight line connecting its two corner heights — turning every strip into a trapezoid. A trapezoid's area is just (average of its two parallel side heights) × width — simple, exact geometry for that shape. The only error left over is however much the TRUE curve bulges away from that straight line within the strip.

Simpson's 1/3 rule — a better-fitting shape: a straight line is a crude approximation to a smooth curve. A PARABOLA (a curved "U" or "∩" shape, i.e. a degree-2 polynomial) hugs a general smooth curve far more closely over a short interval. Simpson's rule takes two neighbouring strips at a time (3 points: left, middle, right), fits the ONE parabola that passes through all three exactly, and uses ITS area instead of the trapezoid's — same amount of data, noticeably less error, because a parabola simply resembles a real curve better than a straight line does.

Romberg integration — squeezing more accuracy out of the CRUDE method, for free: run the (comparatively inaccurate) trapezoidal rule at a coarse resolution and again at a finer one (double the strips). It turns out the ERROR in the trapezoidal rule shrinks in a very predictable, provable mathematical pattern as h halves (roughly by a factor of 4 each time you halve h). Because that pattern is known in advance, you can combine the coarse and fine trapezoidal estimates with a specific weighted formula that mathematically CANCELS the leading error term between them almost entirely — a technique called Richardson extrapolation. The remarkable result: combining two cheap, individually-poor trapezoidal estimates the right way produces an answer as good as (often exactly equal to) Simpson's rule, without ever fitting a parabola directly. Repeat the cancellation trick one level deeper (combining two already-improved estimates) and you squeeze out even more accuracy — that's what building up to R₃,₃ below is doing.

🎯 HOW TO APPROACH IT

Same integral, three methods — compute the node values ONCE (h=(4−1)/6=0.5, 7 nodes) and reuse them for (a) and (b). For (c), Romberg needs its OWN doubling sequence starting from N=1 (h₁=3), NOT the N=6 nodes from parts (a)/(b) — build R₁,₁ → R₂,₁ → R₃,₁ first (trapezoidal at N=1,2,4), then Richardson-extrapolate across to R₃,₃.

🔍 WHY THE WEIGHTS ARE 1, 4, 2, 4, 2, 4, 1

Simpson's rule works on PAIRS of strips at a time (needs an ODD number of points / EVEN number of strips, which is why N must be even). With 7 nodes (f₀…f₆) that's 3 pairs: (f₀,f₁,f₂), (f₂,f₃,f₄), (f₄,f₅,f₆) — each triple gets its own parabola fitted through it. f₂ and f₄ are each the SHARED endpoint between two neighbouring parabolas, so they get counted once by each — that's why they end up weighted ×2. Every ODD-indexed point (f₁, f₃, f₅) sits in the MIDDLE of its own pair and is weighted ×4 (a property of how the parabola's exact area formula distributes weight to its midpoint). The two true endpoints f₀ and f₆ belong to only one parabola each, so they stay at ×1. You don't need to re-derive this pattern — just alternate 4,2,4,2,…,4 between the two endpoints, always ending on a 4 right before the last ×1.
